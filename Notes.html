Chapter 2

1. Byron Katie, Loving What Is: Four Questions That Can Change Your Life (New York: Three Rivers Press, 2003).

2. Interestingly, although Freud is well known for having proposed that the patient “say what comes to mind,” many therapists do not realize that in 1923 Freud wrote one of his major papers in which he pointed out that patients cannot do this. In fact, they spend most of their time talking away from what is important. This led to a major shift in his work: focusing on defenses—the lies we tell ourselves to avoid the pain in our lives.

5. For those wondering about the use of the word “soul,” I recommend Bruno Bettelheim’s book Freud and Man’s Soul. Bettelheim describes how Freud used the word “soul” to refer to the depths of the human person, who we are under the words, what is unknown and yet to be known. In fact, the word “psychoanalysis” does not exist in Freud’s work. The term he used was Seeleanalyse, analysis of the soul.
